# Best Hiking Boots 2026: Top Picks for Every Trail

Choosing the right hiking boots can make or break a national park adventure. Whether you are tackling rocky switchbacks in Zion, splashing through creek crossings in Great Smoky Mountains, or grinding out miles on the Appalachian Trail, your footwear needs to deliver support, traction, and comfort from the first step to the last.

We spent over 200 miles testing eight of the top hiking boots available in 2026 across a variety of terrain types, weather conditions, and trail difficulties. From lightweight speed hikers to burly backpacking boots, this guide covers the best options for every kind of trail and every kind of hiker.

## How to Choose the Right Hiking Boots

Before diving into specific models, it helps to understand the key factors that separate a great boot from a mediocre one.

**Support and Stability.** Ankle-height boots provide lateral stability on uneven terrain, but they also add weight. If you stick to well-maintained trails, a low-cut hiking shoe may be all you need. For off-trail scrambling, talus fields, or heavy pack loads, a mid-cut or high-cut boot is worth the extra ounces.

**Waterproofing.** Gore-Tex and similar membranes keep water out while allowing some breathability. Waterproof boots are essential for shoulder-season hiking, creek crossings, and parks like Olympic or Glacier where rain is a constant companion. The tradeoff is reduced airflow, so desert hikers may prefer non-waterproof models.

**Fit and Break-In.** Modern hiking boots require far less break-in time than the leather monsters of decades past, but fit still matters enormously. Try boots on in the afternoon when your feet are slightly swollen, wear the socks you plan to hike in, and make sure you have about a thumb's width of space in front of your toes.

**Traction.** Look for deep lugs made from sticky rubber compounds like Vibram Megagrip or Contagrip. Lug pattern matters too: multidirectional lugs handle a wider variety of surfaces than simple chevron patterns.

**Weight.** Every ounce on your feet is roughly equivalent to five ounces on your back in terms of energy expenditure. Lighter boots reduce fatigue on long days, but heavier boots often provide better support and durability.

## 1. Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX

**Price:** $165 | [Shop on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B08WNJJNK6?tag=hyroxdatalab-20)

The Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX continues to be one of the most popular hiking boots on the market, and for good reason. This mid-cut boot hits an exceptional balance between lightweight agility and supportive stability that makes it suitable for everything from groomed national park trails to moderately technical scrambles.

The chassis uses Salomon's Advanced Chassis system, a TPU plate embedded in the midsole that provides torsional rigidity without adding stiffness to the forefoot. This means you get excellent stability on rocky terrain while still maintaining a natural, responsive feel on flat ground. The Gore-Tex membrane keeps your feet dry in rain and shallow creek crossings, and the Contagrip MA outsole delivers reliable grip on both wet rock and loose dirt.

Where the X Ultra 4 really shines is in its fit. Salomon's SensiFit construction wraps around the midfoot like a glove, and the lacing system allows precise adjustments from the toe box to the ankle. Out of the box, this boot requires almost zero break-in time. We took them straight from the box to a 14-mile day hike in Rocky Mountain National Park and experienced no hot spots or blisters.

**Best for:** Versatile day hiking on mixed terrain, fast-and-light hikers who want ankle support without bulk.

## 2. Merrell Moab 3 Mid Waterproof

**Price:** $145 | [Shop on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0988BHNHN?tag=hyroxdatalab-20)

The Merrell Moab line has been a bestselling hiking boot for over a decade, earning its reputation as "the out of the box" boot for its legendary comfort from day one. The Moab 3 Mid Waterproof builds on that legacy with subtle refinements that address the few complaints hikers had about the Moab 2.

Merrell widened the toe box slightly in the third generation, which is great news for hikers with broader feet. The Vibram TC5+ outsole provides dependable traction on most surfaces, though it does not grip as aggressively on wet rock as some competitors. The bellows tongue keeps debris out effectively, and the waterproof membrane handles light rain and puddles without issue.

The midsole cushioning uses Merrell's proprietary air cushion in the heel, which absorbs impact on downhill sections and reduces fatigue on long days. The boot weighs in at a reasonable 2 lbs 2 oz per pair for a men's size 9, which puts it in the middle of the pack for mid-cut waterproof boots. If you are looking for a reliable, comfortable boot at a reasonable price point, the Moab 3 remains one of the best values in hiking footwear.

**Best for:** Budget-conscious hikers, wide feet, well-maintained national park trails.

## 3. HOKA Speedgoat 5 Mid GTX

**Price:** $185 | [Shop on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B09YTWLSMH?tag=hyroxdatalab-20)

HOKA brought its maximalist running shoe philosophy to the hiking world with the Speedgoat Mid, and the fifth generation is the most refined version yet. The standout feature is the massive stack height of cushioning underfoot, which provides a plush, almost bouncy feel that dramatically reduces fatigue on long days.

The Vibram Megagrip outsole with 5mm lugs provides outstanding traction on loose gravel, packed dirt, and even wet rock. The Gore-Tex upper keeps water out while the gusseted tongue prevents debris from entering. At just 1 lb 12 oz per pair, the Speedgoat 5 Mid GTX is one of the lightest mid-cut waterproof boots on this list, making it ideal for hikers who want to cover serious distance.

The tradeoff is that the thick, soft midsole can feel slightly unstable on very technical terrain with sharp, angled rocks. Hikers who prioritize ground feel for technical scrambling may prefer a firmer boot. But for the vast majority of national park trails, the Speedgoat's cushioning is a genuine game-changer for comfort.

**Best for:** Long-distance day hikes, hikers with joint issues, fast hiking and trail running crossover.

## 4. La Sportiva Ultra Raptor II Mid GTX

**Price:** $199 | [Shop on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B096C7FH3T?tag=hyroxdatalab-20)

La Sportiva builds gear for serious mountain athletes, and the Ultra Raptor II Mid GTX reflects that pedigree. This boot is built for aggressive terrain, with a burly construction that can handle talus fields, exposed ridgelines, and the kind of rugged scrambling you find in parks like Grand Teton, Glacier, and North Cascades.

The FriXion XF 2.0 outsole is exceptionally sticky on rock, outperforming every other boot on this list in wet rock traction testing. The TPU shank provides rigid support for edging on narrow ledges, and the reinforced toe cap protects against rock strikes. The Gore-Tex lining handles waterproofing duties, and the padded collar provides excellent ankle support.

The fit runs narrow and precise, which is typical of La Sportiva footwear. Hikers with wider feet should try these on before buying or consider going up a half size. The break-in period is slightly longer than some competitors, requiring about 20-30 miles before the boot fully molds to your foot. But once broken in, the Ultra Raptor II Mid is one of the most capable and confidence-inspiring boots you can put on your feet.

**Best for:** Technical terrain, scrambling, mountain hiking, narrow feet.

## 5. Danner Trail 2650 GTX

**Price:** $170 | [Shop on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0CHK143G2?tag=hyroxdatalab-20)

The Danner Trail 2650 was designed by thru-hiking the entire Pacific Crest Trail, and that DNA shows in every design decision. This boot prioritizes lightweight performance and all-day comfort over burly durability, making it an excellent choice for hikers who cover big miles on established trails.

The Trailguard platform provides a flexible, responsive ride that feels more like a trail runner than a traditional hiking boot. The EXO Heel system locks your heel in place without constricting the forefoot, and the OrthoLite footbed provides excellent arch support. The Vibram 460 outsole uses a Megagrip compound with a self-adapting lug pattern that performs well across different surface types.

At 1 lb 10 oz per pair, the Trail 2650 is remarkably light for a waterproof mid-cut boot. The mesh and synthetic upper dries faster than full leather boots, which is a real advantage on multi-day trips. The only downside is that the lightweight construction means less durability over time. Expect to get 500-700 miles from a pair rather than the 1,000+ miles you might get from a burlier boot.

**Best for:** Fast hiking, thru-hiking, lightweight enthusiasts, established trails.

## 6. Columbia Newton Ridge Plus II Waterproof

**Price:** $100 | [Shop on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B08MLD83NN?tag=hyroxdatalab-20)

For hikers on a tight budget, the Columbia Newton Ridge Plus II Waterproof is hard to beat. At around $100, it costs significantly less than most boots on this list while still delivering competent performance on moderate terrain. This is the boot we recommend to first-time hikers who are not sure if they want to invest heavily in hiking footwear just yet.

The seam-sealed waterproof construction handles rain and wet grass effectively, though it is not quite as breathable as Gore-Tex options. The Techlite midsole provides adequate cushioning, and the Omni-Grip rubber outsole offers decent traction on packed dirt and gravel. The full-grain leather and suede upper gives the boot a classic look and reasonable durability.

Where the Newton Ridge cuts corners to hit its price point is in the finer details: the insole is flat and unsupportive compared to premium boots, the lug depth is shallower, and the overall construction is heavier relative to the support it provides. For occasional hikers tackling moderate national park trails, these are perfectly serviceable. For serious hikers planning to rack up hundreds of miles, investing in a higher-end option will pay dividends.

**Best for:** Budget hikers, occasional use, first-time hikers, moderate trails.

## 7. Keen Targhee III Mid Waterproof

**Price:** $155 | [Shop on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B01N23HMEY?tag=hyroxdatalab-20)

Keen has carved out a loyal following among hikers who value a roomy toe box and all-day comfort, and the Targhee III Mid Waterproof delivers on both counts. The signature Keen wide toe box allows your toes to splay naturally, reducing pressure and blisters on long days.

The Keen.Dry waterproof membrane handles wet conditions reliably, and the dual-density EVA midsole with a TPU shank provides a comfortable balance of cushioning and support. The outsole features aggressive, multidirectional lugs that grip well on muddy trails and loose gravel. The padded tongue and collar provide excellent comfort around the ankle without being overly restrictive.

One of the Targhee III's underrated strengths is its environmental credentials. Keen uses a PFC-free durable water repellent treatment and sources leather from environmentally preferred tanneries. The boot is also built on a wider last than most competitors, making it one of the most comfortable options for hikers with wide feet or bunions. If comfort is your top priority and you do not need a featherweight boot, the Targhee III is an outstanding choice.

**Best for:** Wide feet, all-day comfort, wet and muddy trails, environmentally conscious hikers.

## 8. Oboz Bridger Mid BDry

**Price:** $160 | [Shop on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B00FJ2LC56?tag=hyroxdatalab-20)

The Oboz Bridger Mid BDry is a workhorse boot that excels at carrying heavy loads over rugged terrain. If you are planning multi-day backpacking trips in national parks, this boot belongs on your short list. The full-grain nubuck leather upper is among the most durable on this list, and the TPU chassis provides exceptional torsional stability under heavy pack loads.

The BDry waterproof membrane keeps your feet dry in sustained wet conditions, and the dual-density EVA midsole provides firm, supportive cushioning that does not bottom out under load. The Granite Peak outsole features deep, aggressive lugs that bite into loose terrain and provide excellent grip on steep grades. The boot weighs 2 lbs 4 oz per pair, which is on the heavier side, but that weight translates directly into support and durability.

Oboz also plants a tree for every pair of shoes sold, which is a nice bonus for hikers who care about their environmental footprint. The Bridger does require a meaningful break-in period of 30-50 miles, so plan accordingly before a major trip. Once broken in, this boot will carry you and your pack through thousands of miles of backcountry terrain.

**Best for:** Backpacking with heavy loads, rugged terrain, durability-focused hikers.

## How We Tested

All boots were tested over a three-month period across multiple national parks including Rocky Mountain, Great Smoky Mountains, Shenandoah, and Zion. Each boot was worn for a minimum of 25 miles on a variety of terrain types including packed dirt, loose gravel, wet rock, mud, and snow. We evaluated traction, waterproofing, comfort, support, weight, and break-in time. Temperature conditions ranged from 28 to 95 degrees Fahrenheit.

## Final Recommendations

For most hikers tackling national park trails, the **Salomon X Ultra 4 GTX** is our top overall pick for its exceptional balance of performance, comfort, and versatility. Budget hikers should look at the **Columbia Newton Ridge Plus II**, while serious backpackers will appreciate the load-carrying capability of the **Oboz Bridger Mid BDry**. Hikers with wide feet should prioritize the **Keen Targhee III**, and those covering big miles will love the cushioning of the **HOKA Speedgoat 5 Mid GTX**.
